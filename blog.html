HOW DONUTS BECAME A STAPLE OF AMERICAN BREAKFAST CULTURE

While donuts were initially consumed as a snack or dessert, they gradually became associated with breakfast. This shift was influenced by several factors, including the rise of coffee culture in the 20th century. As coffee shops proliferated, donuts became a popular pairing with coffee due to their sweetness and ease of consumption. In the mid-20th century, the expansion of donut chains like Dunkin’ Donuts played a crucial role in solidifying the donut as a breakfast staple. Founded in 1950 by William Rosenberg in Quincy, Massachusetts , Dunkin’ Donuts focused on serving fresh coffee and donuts to busy workers and commuters. The chain’s emphasis on convenience and consistency appealed to the fast-paced lifestyle of post-war America, where people increasingly sought quick and portable breakfast options. Marketing campaigns by donut chains also contributed to the perception of donuts as a breakfast food. Dunkin’ Donuts, for example, famously used the slogan “America Runs on Dunkin’,” reinforcing the idea that donuts and coffee were essential to starting the day. This messaging resonated with the public, and the combination of donuts and coffee became a morning ritual for many Americans. Donuts became associated with coffee in American culture due to several interrelated factors that transformed them into a popular breakfast pairing.
